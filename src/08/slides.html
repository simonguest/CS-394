Ethics: Bias and Fairness

Bias and Fairness

  • Training data (used for pretraining) often contain societal biases.
  • These biases influence how models respond
    • Sometimes in an explicit way, but often in more subtle ways or where input data contains diverse demographic details.
  • Why it happens
    • Not accidental malice, but a mathematical inevitability
    • Models learn statistical patterns from data. If the data reflects historical inequities, the model will too.

Bias and Fairness

  • Examples:
    • Google Gemini’s Image Generation (Feb 2024): Google’s Gemini model was generating historically inaccurate images — e.g., racially diverse Nazi soldiers — when users asked for images of historical figures.
    • Workday AI Hiring Bias Lawsuit — Mobley v. Workday (2023–2025): A Black job seeker over 40 with a disability sued Workday, alleging its AI applicant-screening system systematically rejected him based on race, age, and disability.
    • AI Salary Negotiation Bias (2025): Researchers found that when LLMs including GPT-4o mini, Claude 3.5 Haiku, and others were asked to advise on salary negotiations, the chatbots recommended lower salaries to women and minority candidates compared to equally qualified white men.

Bias and Fairness

  • How can we mitigate?
    • Before training:
      • Sourcing of more balanced and representative datasets, intentionally oversampling underrepresented groups. Auditing for bias prior to training.
    • During Training:
      • “Fairness constraints” built into the loss function. Mathematically elegant, but definition of “fair” can be subjective.
    • Alignment (e.g., RLHF):
      • Where most of the foundational models focus their efforts. Human feedback to identify and down-weight biased output.
      • Anthropic’s Constitutional AI is a good example.

Bias and Fairness

  • Inference Time:
    • e.g., Injecting into the system prompt. Most brittle and non-deterministic.
  • Auditing and Red-Teaming:
    • Independent bias audits to probe models for disparate outcomes across demographic groups.
    • e.g., Google’s PAIR “What-If Tool”
    • IBM AI Fairness 360

Ethics: Explainability

Explainability

  • “Black box” models raise ethical concerns in high-stakes domains such as healthcare, criminal justice, military
  • If/when things go wrong, it can be challenging to audit the output and/or reasoning from a model
  • The over-confidence of today’s LLMs can compound the issue

Explainability

  • Examples
    • Healthcare: In a survey of clinicians using LLM-based diagnostic tools, 68% were uncomfortable with the inability to follow traceable reasoning paths — an essential requirement for legal accountability and shared decision-making with patients.
    • Legal Briefs: In 2023, a New York lawyer submitted a legal brief to a federal court that had been generated largely by ChatGPT. The judge noted the brief was littered with “bogus judicial decisions, bogus quotes and bogus internal citations”
    • Legal Decision Making: In June 2024, the Shenzhen Intermediate Court in China unveiled an Intelligent Adjudication System. This is the first instance globally of a court methodically using an LLM to assist judges in deciding cases, covering family law, contracts, torts, and labor disputes.

Explainability

  • How can we mitigate?
    • Chain-of-Thought and Reasoning Models:
      • Prompting or training LLMs to show their reasoning step-by-step before reaching a conclusion makes the logic auditable, even if the underlying mechanism remains opaque.
      • A clinician can at least see “the model considered these symptoms, ruled out these conditions, and arrived at this recommendation” rather than just receiving an output.
    • Retrieval Augmented Generateion (RAG):
      • Rather than relying on an LLM’s parametric memory, RAG grounds responses in specific retrieved documents. E.g., clinical guidelines, case law, medical literature.
      • This doesn’t make the model itself explainable, but it makes the sources traceable.
    • Human-in-the-Loop Requirements
      • Regulatory and procedural frameworks that mandate human review before any LLM output becomes a decision. (The EU AI Act essentially requires this for high-risk domains.)

Explainability

  • Domain-Specific Fine-Tuning and Evaluation
    • Rather than deploying general-purpose LLMs in clinical or legal settings, fine-tuning on domain-specific, high-quality data and then rigorously red-teaming against known failure cases.
    • The Shenzhen court system is an example — they used a purpose-trained model rather than off-the-shelf ChatGPT.
  • Confidence Calibration and Uncertainty Quantification
    • Another training/fine-tuning opportunity to train models to express uncertainty: e.g., “I’m not confident about this” vs. stating everything with conviction.
    • An active research area, although current LLMs remain notoriously over-confident.

Ethics: Manipulative Content

Manipulative Content

  • Deep fakes (synthetic media), exploitation of psychological vulnerabilities

Manipulative Content

  • Examples
    • Personal:
      • Scammers are using AI voice cloning tools to impersonate family members in distress, requiring only a few seconds of audio scraped from social media.
      • In a recent Florida case, a mother sent $15,000 after receiving a call from someone using her daughter’s cloned voice claiming to be in an accident. She later discovered her daughter was safe and had never made the call.

TBD: Could I do a demo of voice cloning?

  • Financial:
    • In Jan 2024, fraudsters using deepfake technology impersonated a company’s CFO on a video call, tricking an employee into transferring $25 million.
    • The victim joined what appeared to be a legitimate multi-person video conference with colleagues and their CFO, all of whom were deepfake renderings.
  • Political:
    • A 2024 report by Recorded Future documented 82 pieces of AI-generated deepfake content targeting public figures across 38 countries in a single year
    • A survey conducted by the identity verification firm Jumio found that 72% of Americans believed deepfakes would influence elections

Manipulative Content

  • How can we mitigate?
    • Individual level: The “family safe word” has emerged as the most widely recommended mitigation for voice cloning scams specifically. i.e., a pre-agreed word or phrase that only genuine family members would know, used to verify identity before sending money.
    • Deepfake Detection Tech: An active area of research and development, but fundamentally a cat and mouse problem. Detection models are often trained on previous generations of fakes, and generative models tend to outpace them.
    • Cryptographic Watermarks: The Coalition for Content Provenance and Authenticity (C2PA) is developing open standards for embedding verifiable metadata into media at the point of creation (a cryptographic “certificate of authenticity” that travels with the file.) Major camera manufacturers, Adobe, and several AI companies have signed on.
    • Regulatory: The US TAKE IT DOWN Act (signed May 2025) criminalizes non-consensual intimate deepfakes at the federal level. The EU AI Act’s deepfake labeling requirements became mandatory in August 2025. Enforcement is of course challenging.

Labor and Socioeconomic

  • Examples
    • Corporate Restructuring: In 2025–early 2026, major companies across tech and manufacturing cited AI as a reason for layoffs and workforce restructuring. In 2025, research firms attributed 17,000+ job cuts to AI, and another 20,000 to technological updates that likely include AI.

IP: Training Data and Copyright

Training Data and Copyright

  • TBD Does training on copyrighted work constitute infringement?

Training Data and Copyright

  • Examples
    • New York Times: In late 2025, The New York Times filed a federal copyright infringement lawsuit against OpenAI and its partner Microsoft.
      • The Times alleges that OpenAI scraped and used millions of its articles, including behind paywalls, without permission to train the ChatGPT language models that power services like ChatGPT and Bing AI.
      • This case is one of the most high-profile disputes over AI training on copyrighted news content, and it’s still active in federal court.
    • Getty Images: Getty Images sued Stability AI in UK and Europe alleging that its generative image model (e.g., Stable Diffusion) was trained on millions of Getty’s copyrighted photographs without permission.
      • The claim focused on the unlicensed use of high-value image assets as training data and argued that elements like watermarks showing up in outputs indicated the model had learned from copyrighted photos.
    • Anthropic: Anthropic agreed to pay about $1.5 billion to settle copyright claims that it had trained its models on millions of authors’ works, including allegedly pirated copies used in model training.
      • The case included acquired books that Anthropic purchased: In some cases the company bought physical copies, digitally scanned them (“destructive digitization”), and used those scans in model training.

Training Data and Copyright

  • Mitigations
    • Robots.txt: Authors who publish online can add directives in their site’s robots.txt file to block known AI crawlers. (Voluntary compliance only)

      • Example:

        User-agent: GPTBot
Disallow: /
    • Technical Poisioning/Watermarking:
      • Embed invisible watermarks. Challenging as these watermarks will degrade via the diffusion process.
      • Add adversarial pertubations (“defensive” and “poisioning”) to images to degrade model training.
        • “Glaze” and “Nightshade” from UChicago
        • Adds visual distortions based on a strength setting, with tags (e.g., hats become cars). Lowest strengths barely noticable side-by-side.

  • Legal Tools
    • Copyright registration and explicit licensing terms
    • Potential to negotiate AI clauses in publishing contracts
    • But, courts are still determining if AI training consistutes fair use
  • Future Directions
    • Dataset transparency laws
    • Collective licensing systems (similar to music royalties)

Model Weights As IP

  • Examples
    • Getty Images: In Nov 2025, the UK High Court explicitly addressed whether an AI model’s learned parameters (weights) constitute an infringing copy of copyrighted works used in training.
      • The judge held that Stable Diffusion’s model weights do not store or contain reproductions of Getty’s copyrighted images, and therefore the model itself is not an “infringing copy” under UK copyright law*.
    • Rothwell Figg: Few active cases in court, but companies routinely treat model weights as deeply valuable trade secrets, and this strategy is now being recognized as a major form of IP protection in industry and law.
      • Legal analyses (and filings) note that model weights, parameters, training datasets and processes are prime candidates for trade secret protection, because they derive economic value from being secret and companies take “reasonable steps” to keep them confidential.
    • Most legal experts agree that model weights are not copyrightable under current U.S. & UK copyright law because they are functional numeric parameters, not expressive works.
      • To be copyrighted, a work must be a copyable expression with human authorship.
    • Anthropic: In Feb 2026, Anthropic publicly announced it had identified “industrial-scale campaigns” by three Chinese AI companies: DeepSeek, Moonshot AI, and MiniMax. They allegedly interacted with its Claude model tens of millions of times in order to extract its capabilities for training their own models.
      • These companies used “distillation” (where a model learns from a more capable model), creating roughly 24,000 fraudulent accounts and made over 16 million prompt interactions with Claude to mine its outputs.
      • Irony not lost on social networks: “Why is it an attack? If they scraped my blog for training their model, is it attack on the blog? Isn’t that exactly like training on copyrighted materials?”
      • Anthropic’s ToS prohibit Claude’s outputs to “build a competing product or service, including to train competing AI models” (https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=44763110)

Output Ownership

  • Examples
    • Zarya of the Dawn: In 2022–2023, the U.S. Copyright Office initially granted copyright registration to the comic. After discovering the images were generated using Midjourney, the Office partially revoked the registration.
      • Key ruling: The text and selection/arrangement of panels were copyrightable. The AI-generated images themselves were not, because they lacked sufficient human authorship.
    • Jason Allen: A Colorado-based artist who became widely known in 2022 after winning first place in the digital art category of the Colorado State Fair (with a piece created using an AI image generator)
      • TBD: Picture link here
      • Produced through iterative prompting (624 prompts) using the text-to-image system Midjourney, followed by compositional editing in Photoshop and upscaling in Gigapixel AI.
      • Critics argued that AI-generated images challenge traditional notions of creativity, while supporters contended that prompt engineering and artistic intent remain essential human contributions.
      • In 2022, Allen applied for copyright registration of the image with US Copyright Office, which was later denied after appeal.
    • US Copyright Office AI Policy Guidance: The Copyright Office released detailed guidance (in their 2024-2025 update) clarifying a spectrum model:
      • Fully autonomous AI output: Not copyrightable
      • AI + human curation/editing: Possibly copyrightable
      • AI as assistive tool (e.g., Photoshop-level control): Copyrightable
